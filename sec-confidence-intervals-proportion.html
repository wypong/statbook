    Section 10.3 Confidence Intervals for a Population Proportion

    Many statistical questions are really yes-or-no questions. What proportion of voters support a ballot measure? What proportion of products coming off an assembly line are defective? What proportion of students at a college work more than 20 hours per week? In settings like these, the parameter we want is the population proportion, usually written \(p\text{.}\)
    🔗
    If we take a random sample of size \(n\) and count the number of successes, say \(X\text{,}\) then a natural estimate of \(p\) is the sample proportion:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \widehat{p}=\frac{X}{n}. \end{equation*}
    This section is built directly on the binomial model from Section 8.4. If each observation is a success or failure, and the success probability is \(p\text{,}\) then the count \(X\) of successes in \(n\) independent trials has a binomial distribution:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} X\sim B(n,p). \end{equation*}
    From the binomial chapter, for large enough \(n\) we can approximate this binomial count by a normal random variable with the same mean and standard deviation:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} X \approx N(np,\sqrt{np(1-p)}), \end{equation*}
    provided the expected numbers of successes and failures are both at least about 5:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} np\ge 5 \qquad\text{and}\qquad n(1-p)\ge 5. \end{equation*}
    Now divide everything by \(n\text{.}\) Since \(\widehat{p}=X/n\text{,}\) the sample proportion is approximately normal with mean
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} E(\widehat{p})=E\left(\frac{X}{n}\right)=\frac{E(X)}{n}=\frac{np}{n}=p \end{equation*}
    and standard deviation
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \sigma_{\widehat{p}} =\frac{\sigma_X}{n} =\frac{\sqrt{np(1-p)}}{n} =\sqrt{\frac{p(1-p)}{n}}. \end{equation*}
    So for large samples,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \widehat{p}\approx N\left(p,\sqrt{\frac{p(1-p)}{n}}\right). \end{equation*}
    That is the key fact behind a one-proportion confidence interval. Once we standardize, we get the approximate z-statistic
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\widehat{p}-p}{\sqrt{p(1-p)/n}} \approx N(0,1). \end{equation*}
    Let \(\alpha=1-C\) as before. Then the middle proportion \(C\) of the standard normal curve gives
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} P\left( -z_{\alpha/2} \lt \frac{\widehat{p}-p}{\sqrt{p(1-p)/n}} \lt z_{\alpha/2} \right)\approx C. \end{equation*}
    Solving that inequality for \(p\) suggests the interval
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \widehat{p}\pm z_{\alpha/2}\sqrt{\frac{p(1-p)}{n}}. \end{equation*}
    There is one catch: the standard deviation still contains the unknown parameter \(p\text{.}\) In practice we plug in the sample proportion \(\widehat{p}\) in its place. That gives the usual large-sample confidence interval for a population proportion:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \widehat{p}\pm z_{\alpha/2}\sqrt{\frac{\widehat{p}(1-\widehat{p})}{n}}. \end{equation*}
    So the margin of error for a proportion is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} E=z_{\alpha/2}\sqrt{\frac{\widehat{p}(1-\widehat{p})}{n}}. \end{equation*}
    This method works well when the sample is random or otherwise independent, and when the sample is large enough that the observed counts of successes and failures are both at least about 5:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} n\widehat{p}\ge 5 \qquad\text{and}\qquad n(1-\widehat{p})\ge 5. \end{equation*}
    If sampling is done without replacement from a finite population, a common rule of thumb is that the population should be at least 10 times as large as the sample so the observations are close to independent.
    🔗

    Example 10.3.1. A Confidence Interval for Support in a Campus Poll.

    A random sample of 200 students is asked whether they support a new campus tutoring center. Suppose 124 students say yes. Estimate the population proportion \(p\) of all students who support the plan with 95% confidence.
    🔗
    Here \(x=124\text{,}\) \(n=200\text{,}\) so
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \widehat{p}=\frac{124}{200}=0.62. \end{equation*}
    The large-sample conditions are met because
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} n\widehat{p}=200(0.62)=124 \qquad\text{and}\qquad n(1-\widehat{p})=200(0.38)=76, \end{equation*}
    and both numbers are much larger than 5. For 95% confidence, \(z_{0.025}=1.96\text{.}\) The margin of error is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} E=1.96\sqrt{\frac{0.62(0.38)}{200}}\approx 0.0673. \end{equation*}
    So the confidence interval is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} 0.62\pm 0.0673=(0.5527,0.6873). \end{equation*}
    So we estimate that between about 55.3% and 68.7% of all students support the tutoring center.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 10.3.2. A Confidence Interval for a Defect Rate.

    A quality-control inspector checks 400 light bulbs and finds 18 defective ones. Find a 90% confidence interval for the population proportion of defective bulbs.
    🔗
    First compute the sample proportion:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \widehat{p}=\frac{18}{400}=0.045. \end{equation*}
    The large-sample conditions are satisfied because
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} n\widehat{p}=400(0.045)=18 \qquad\text{and}\qquad n(1-\widehat{p})=400(0.955)=382. \end{equation*}
    For 90% confidence, \(z_{0.05}=1.645\text{.}\) The margin of error is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} E=1.645\sqrt{\frac{0.045(0.955)}{400}}\approx 0.0170. \end{equation*}
    So the confidence interval is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} 0.045\pm 0.0170=(0.0280,0.0620). \end{equation*}
    That puts the defect rate at about 2.8% to 6.2%.
    🔗
    🔗
    The interpretation follows the same pattern as for means. A 95% confidence interval for \(p\) does not mean there is a 95% chance that the fixed population proportion is inside the one interval we already computed. It means that if we repeated the sampling process many times and built an interval each time, about 95% of those intervals would capture the true proportion.
    🔗
