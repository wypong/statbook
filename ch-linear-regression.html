    Chapter 5 Linear Regression

    So far we have mostly studied one variable at a time. But in practice, statistics often asks whether two variables move together. Do students who study more hours tend to earn higher exam scores? Do larger apartments usually cost more rent? Does a car with more mileage usually sell for less?
    🔗
    Questions like these lead to two-variable statistics. In this chapter we look at how to display paired data with a scatterplot, how to describe the direction and strength of a linear relationship, and how to model that relationship with a linear regression line.
    🔗
    • 5.1 Scatterplots and Correlation
    • 5.2 Regression Lines and Prediction
    • 5.3 Exercises

    Summary.

    This chapter introduced linear regression through two-variable data. The first step is to display paired data with a scatterplot. The next step is to describe whether the pattern is positive, negative, weak, or strong. Finally, we can use a regression line to summarize the linear trend and make predictions.
    🔗
    • Two-variable statistics studies pairs of observations \((x,y)\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • A scatterplot is the standard graph for paired numerical data.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Positive correlation means larger \(x\) values tend to go with larger \(y\) values. Negative correlation means larger \(x\) values tend to go with smaller \(y\) values.
      🔗
      🔗
    • The correlation coefficient \(r\) measures the direction and strength of a linear relationship.
      🔗
      🔗
    • The value of \(r\) always lies between \(-1\) and \(1\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • A regression line has the form \(\hat{y}=a+bx\) and is used to model and predict \(y\) from \(x\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • The slope describes how the predicted value changes when \(x\) increases by 1 unit.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Interpolation is usually safer than extrapolation.
      🔗
      🔗
    Linear regression is one of the simplest modeling tools in statistics, but it is also one of the most useful. It helps us move from description to prediction, while reminding us to look carefully at the data before trusting a formula.
