    Section 6.3 Conditional Probability and Independence

    Sometimes the probability of an event changes once we learn extra information. That idea leads to conditional probability. In this section we will compute conditional probabilities directly, organize them with contingency tables, and use them to decide whether two events are independent.
    🔗

    Subsection Conditional Probability

    The probability of \(B\) given \(A\) is written \(P(B\mid A)\text{.}\) It means the probability that the outcome is in \(B\) once we already know the outcome is in \(A\text{.}\) If \(P(A)\ne 0\text{,}\) then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} P(B\mid A)=\frac{P(A\cap B)}{P(A)}. \end{equation*}
    This formula can be rearranged into the multiplication rule:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} P(A\cap B)=P(B\mid A)P(A). \end{equation*}

    Example 6.3.1. Conditional Probability on a Die Roll.

    Suppose a fair die is rolled. Let \(A=\{1,3,5\}\) be the event “the outcome is odd,” and let \(B=\{3,4,5\}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Without extra information,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} P(B)=\frac{3}{6}=\frac{1}{2}. \end{equation*}
    But if we know the outcome is in \(A\text{,}\) then only 1, 3, and 5 are still possible. Among those three odd outcomes, two are in \(B\text{.}\) So
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} P(B\mid A)=\frac{2}{3}. \end{equation*}
    Using the formula gives the same answer:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} P(B\mid A)=\frac{P(A\cap B)}{P(A)}=\frac{2/6}{3/6}=\frac{2}{3}. \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Subsection Independence

    Two events \(A\) and \(B\) are independent if knowing that one occurred does not change the probability of the other. In symbols, independence means
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} P(B\mid A)=P(B), \end{equation*}
    which is equivalent to
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} P(A\cap B)=P(A)P(B). \end{equation*}

    Example 6.3.2. Checking Independence.

    Let \(A=\{1,3,5\}\) be the event “the die shows an odd number,” and let \(B=\{1,2,3,4\}\) be the event “the die shows a number at most 4.”
    🔗
    Then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} P(A)=\frac{3}{6}=\frac{1}{2} \qquad\text{and}\qquad P(B)=\frac{4}{6}=\frac{2}{3}. \end{equation*}
    The intersection is \(A\cap B=\{1,3\}\text{,}\) so
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} P(A\cap B)=\frac{2}{6}=\frac{1}{3}. \end{equation*}
    Because
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} P(A)P(B)=\frac{1}{2}\cdot\frac{2}{3}=\frac{1}{3}=P(A\cap B), \end{equation*}
    the events are independent.
    🔗
    🔗
    It is important not to mix up independence and mutual exclusivity. They are different ideas.
    🔗
    • Mutually exclusive events cannot happen together.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Independent events can happen together, but one does not affect the probability of the other.
      🔗
      🔗
    If two events have positive probability and are mutually exclusive, then they cannot be independent, because their intersection has probability 0 while the product \(P(A)P(B)\) is positive.
    🔗

    Subsection Contingency Tables

    A contingency table, also called a two-way table, organizes counts for two categorical variables at the same time. It is one of the most useful ways to compute conditional probabilities from real data.
    🔗
    The counts inside the table are joint frequencies because each count belongs to a combination of two categories. The totals along the edges are marginal totals. The grand total is the total number of observations.
    🔗

    Example 6.3.3. Conditional Probability from a Contingency Table.

    A class survey asked 80 students whether they usually take public transportation to campus and whether their commute is at least 30 minutes. The results are shown in Table 6.3.4.
    🔗
    🔗
    Let \(L\) be the event “the commute is at least 30 minutes” and let \(T\) be the event “the student uses public transportation.” From the table,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} P(L)=\frac{40}{80},\qquad P(T)=\frac{30}{80},\qquad P(L\cap T)=\frac{18}{80}. \end{equation*}
    To find \(P(T\mid L)\text{,}\) restrict attention to the row for commutes at least 30 minutes. Out of those 40 students, 18 use public transportation, so
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} P(T\mid L)=\frac{18}{40}=0.45. \end{equation*}
    To find \(P(L\mid T)\text{,}\) restrict attention to the column for public transportation. Out of those 30 students, 18 have commutes at least 30 minutes, so
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} P(L\mid T)=\frac{18}{30}=0.60. \end{equation*}
    This is a good reminder that \(P(T\mid L)\) and \(P(L\mid T)\) usually answer different questions. The condition tells us which row or column becomes the new total.
    🔗
    Contingency tables also help us check independence. In this example, \(P(T)=30/80=0.375\text{,}\) but \(P(T\mid L)=0.45\text{.}\) Since knowing that the commute is at least 30 minutes changes the probability that a student uses public transportation, the events \(L\) and \(T\) are not independent.
    🔗
