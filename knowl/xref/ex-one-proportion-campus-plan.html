Example 11.4.2. Testing a Claimed Support Level.

A college newspaper reports that 60% of students support a new parking plan. A random sample of 200 students finds that 108 support the plan. Test the newspaper’s claim at the 5% significance level.
🔗
Let \(p\) be the true proportion of all students who support the plan. Since we want to know whether the reported value is off in either direction, we test
🔗
\begin{equation*} H_0:p=0.60 \qquad\text{and}\qquad H_a:p\ne 0.60. \end{equation*}
The sample proportion is
🔗
\begin{equation*} \widehat{p}=\frac{108}{200}=0.54. \end{equation*}
The null-model counts are
🔗
\begin{equation*} np_0=200(0.60)=120 \qquad\text{and}\qquad n(1-p_0)=200(0.40)=80, \end{equation*}
so the normal approximation is reasonable. The test statistic is
🔗
\begin{equation*} z=\frac{0.54-0.60}{\sqrt{0.60(0.40)/200}}\approx -1.73. \end{equation*}
For a two-tailed test, the p-value is
🔗
\begin{equation*} 2P(Z\ge 1.73)\approx 0.0836. \end{equation*}
Since \(0.0836>0.05\text{,}\) we fail to reject \(H_0\text{.}\) At the 5% level, the sample does not give enough evidence to conclude that the true support level differs from 60%.
