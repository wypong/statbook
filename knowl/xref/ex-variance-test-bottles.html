Example 11.5.2. Testing Whether a Machine’s Variation Has Changed.

Assume bottle fill amounts are normally distributed. A machine is supposed to have population standard deviation 1.2 ounces. A random sample of 12 bottles gives sample standard deviation \(s=1.8\) ounces. Test at the 5% significance level whether the standard deviation differs from 1.2 ounces.
🔗
We test
🔗
\begin{equation*} H_0:\sigma=1.2 \qquad\text{and}\qquad H_a:\sigma\ne 1.2. \end{equation*}
Equivalently, \(H_0:\sigma^2=1.44\) and \(H_a:\sigma^2\ne 1.44\text{.}\) The test statistic has \(df=12-1=11\text{:}\)
🔗
\begin{equation*} \chi^2=\frac{(12-1)(1.8)^2}{(1.2)^2}=24.75. \end{equation*}
For a two-tailed test with \(\alpha=0.05\) and \(df=11\text{,}\) the rejection region is
🔗
\begin{equation*} \chi^2<\chi^2_{0.975}[11] \qquad\text{or}\qquad \chi^2>\chi^2_{0.025}[11]. \end{equation*}
From Appendix D,
🔗
\begin{equation*} \chi^2_{0.975}[11]\approx 3.816 \qquad\text{and}\qquad \chi^2_{0.025}[11]\approx 21.920. \end{equation*}
Since \(24.75>21.920\text{,}\) the observed statistic falls in the rejection region, so we reject \(H_0\text{.}\) The data suggest that the machine’s standard deviation differs from 1.2 ounces, and in fact it appears to be larger.
