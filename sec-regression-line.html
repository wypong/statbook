    Section 5.2 Regression Lines and Prediction

    A linear relation has the form
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y=ax+b, \end{equation*}
    where \(a\) is the slope and \(b\) is the y-intercept. If \(a>0\text{,}\) the line goes up as \(x\) increases. If \(a<0\text{,}\) the line goes down as \(x\) increases.
    🔗
    In real data, the points usually do not lie exactly on a line. Instead we choose a line that fits the pattern as well as possible. That line is called the best-fit line or regression line. Its equation is often written
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \hat{y}=a+bx. \end{equation*}
    Here \(\hat{y}\) is the predicted value of \(y\) for a given value of \(x\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Example 5.2.2. Using a Regression Equation.

    Suppose a regression line for study hours and exam score is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \hat{y}=53.5+4.4x. \end{equation*}
    If a student studies for 5 hours, the predicted exam score is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \hat{y}=53.5+4.4(5)=75.5. \end{equation*}
    So the model predicts a score of about 76.
    🔗
    🔗
    The slope tells us how much the predicted \(y\) changes when \(x\) increases by 1 unit. In the example above, the slope is 4.4, so the model predicts that each additional hour of study is associated with about 4.4 more exam points.
    🔗
    The sign of the slope matches the sign of the correlation coefficient \(r\text{.}\) That is useful, but the two numbers are not the same. The slope measures change in the original units, while \(r\) is a unit-free measure of linear strength and always stays between \(-1\) and \(1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Regression is helpful for prediction, but it should be used with care. Predicting for an \(x\) value inside the range of the observed data is called interpolation. Predicting far outside the observed range is called extrapolation, and that is usually much less reliable.
    🔗

    Example 5.2.3. Interpolation Versus Extrapolation.

    If our study-hours data only covers values from 1 to 8 hours, then predicting the score for 6 hours is interpolation. Predicting the score for 20 hours is extrapolation. The line might give a numerical answer, but there is no guarantee that the same pattern continues that far.
    🔗
    🔗

    Practice using a regression equation to make predictions in context.
    🔗

    (a)

    Suppose a regression line for study hours and exam score is \(\hat{y}=53.5+4.4x\text{.}\) Find the predicted score for a student who studies for 4 hours.

    🔗

    (b)

    Use the same equation to predict the score for a student who studies for 7 hours.
    🔗
    🔗

    (c)

    In one sentence, explain what the prediction means in the context of the data.
