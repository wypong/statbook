    Section 9.1 Continuous Random Variables and the Uniform Model

    A random variable is continuous if its possible values fill an interval instead of landing in a countable list. A person’s height is a standard example. So is the amount of time until the next customer arrives.
    🔗
    For a continuous random variable, probabilities come from intervals, not isolated points. The easiest model to start with is the uniform distribution.
    🔗

    Example 9.1.1. An Arrival Time During Office Hours.

    Suppose a student arrives to office hours at a completely random time between noon and 1:00 p.m., and every time in that hour is equally likely. Let \(X\) be the arrival time, measured in minutes after noon.
    🔗
    Then \(X\) is uniformly distributed on the interval \([0,60]\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Now ask a sequence of questions.
    🔗
    1. What is \(P(0\le X\le 30)\text{?}\) Since this interval has length 30 out of a total length of 60, the probability is \(30/60=0.5\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. What is \(P(15\le X\le 45)\text{?}\) This interval also has length 30, so its probability is again \(0.5\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. What is \(P(20\le X\le 35)\text{?}\) Now the interval has length 15, so the probability is \(15/60=0.25\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    4. What is \(P(X=30)\text{,}\) meaning the student arrives exactly at 12:30?
      🔗
      🔗
    The first three questions suggest the right idea: in a uniform model, probability depends on the length of an interval. But a single point has length 0. So
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} P(X=30)=0. \end{equation*}
    The same reasoning shows that \(P(X=a)=0\) for every specific value \(a\text{.}\) A continuous random variable can certainly take the value \(a\text{;}\) it is just that one exact point carries no area and therefore no probability mass by itself.
    🔗
    🔗
    More generally, if \(X\) is uniformly distributed on \([a,b]\text{,}\) then for any interval \([c,d]\) inside \([a,b]\text{,}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} P(c\le X\le d)=\frac{d-c}{b-a}. \end{equation*}
    This formula says exactly what the office-hours example suggested: probability is proportional to interval length.
    🔗
    A probability density function, or PDF, is a function whose area over an interval gives probability. For the uniform distribution on \([a,b]\text{,}\) the density is constant:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f(x)=\frac{1}{b-a}\qquad\text{for }a\le x\le b. \end{equation*}
    That is why the graph is just a rectangle. Probability is area, so for a continuous random variable, a single point has probability 0.
    🔗

    Activity 9.1.1. Food Truck Arrival Window.

    Use a simple arrival-time model to practice probability on a uniform interval.
    🔗

    (a)

    A food truck arrives uniformly at random between 11:00 and 11:30. Let \(X\) be the number of minutes after 11:00. Find \(P(0\le X\le 6)\) and \(P(10\le X\le 18)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    (b)

    Find \(P(X=12)\) and explain why the answer is zero.
    🔗
    🔗

    (c)

    Compare \(P(2\le X\le 8)\) and \(P(20\le X\le 26)\text{.}\) What do the two probabilities have in common?

    Activity 9.1.2. Equal-Length Interval Challenge.

    See why equal-length intervals have equal probability in a uniform model.
    🔗

    (a)

    Suppose \(X\) is uniformly distributed on \([0,60]\text{.}\) Find the probability of any interval of length 10, such as \([5,15]\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    (b)

    Find the probability of another interval of length 10, such as \([30,40]\text{.}\) Compare your answer with the first one.

    In one sentence, explain the general rule for probabilities in a uniform distribution.
