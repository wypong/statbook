    Section 11.8 Tests for a Population Variance and Standard Deviation

    This section is the testing counterpart to Section 10.4. The goal is to decide whether the sample spread gives enough evidence against a claimed value of the population variance \(\sigma^2\) or the population standard deviation \(\sigma\text{.}\)
    🔗
    If a claim is written in terms of standard deviation, we can square both sides and test variance instead. So
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} H_0:\sigma=\sigma_0 \qquad\Longleftrightarrow\qquad H_0:\sigma^2=\sigma_0^2. \end{equation*}
    The alternative can again be left-tailed, right-tailed, or two-tailed:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} H_a:\sigma^2<\sigma_0^2,\qquad H_a:\sigma^2>\sigma_0^2,\qquad H_a:\sigma^2\ne \sigma_0^2. \end{equation*}
    These tests require careful assumptions.
    🔗
    • The data should come from a random sample or a similarly independent process.
      🔗
      🔗
    • The observations should be independent.
      🔗
      🔗
    • The population should be normal. This is the crucial assumption.
      🔗
      🔗
    That last condition matters much more here than it does for tests about a mean. A large sample by itself does not justify the chi-square test for variance. The method depends on the population distribution being normal, not just on the sample size being large.
    🔗
    From Section 10.4, if \(H_0:\sigma^2=\sigma_0^2\) is true and the population is normal, then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \chi^2=\frac{(n-1)S^2}{\sigma_0^2}\sim \chi^2_{n-1}. \end{equation*}
    This gives the chi-square test for a population variance. The interpretation is straightforward:
    🔗
    • If the sample variance \(s^2\) is much larger than the null value \(\sigma_0^2\text{,}\) then the observed chi-square statistic will be large.
      🔗
      🔗
    • If the sample variance \(s^2\) is much smaller than the null value \(\sigma_0^2\text{,}\) then the observed chi-square statistic will be small.
      🔗
      🔗
    So the tail of the test matches the direction of the alternative.
    🔗
    Let \(W\sim \chi^2[n-1]\) when \(H_0\) is true, and let \(\chi^2_{\text{obs}}\) be the observed test statistic.
    🔗
    • For \(H_a:\sigma^2>\sigma_0^2\text{,}\) use the right-tail p-value \(P(W\ge \chi^2_{\text{obs}})\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • For \(H_a:\sigma^2<\sigma_0^2\text{,}\) use the left-tail p-value \(P(W\le \chi^2_{\text{obs}})\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • For \(H_a:\sigma^2\ne \sigma_0^2\text{,}\) reject at significance level \(\alpha\) when \(\chi^2_{\text{obs}}<\chi^2_{1-\alpha/2}[n-1]\) or \(\chi^2_{\text{obs}}>\chi^2_{\alpha/2}[n-1]\text{.}\) Because the chi-square distribution is not symmetric, technology is often the easiest way to find a two-sided p-value.
      🔗
      🔗

    Example 11.8.1. Testing Whether Delivery Times Have Become More Variable.

    Suppose delivery times on a short route are well modeled by a normal distribution. A company claims the population standard deviation is 2 minutes. A random sample of 16 deliveries gives sample standard deviation \(s=2.9\) minutes. Test at the 5% significance level whether the true standard deviation is greater than 2 minutes.
    🔗
    Let \(\sigma\) be the population standard deviation of delivery times. Then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} H_0:\sigma=2 \qquad\text{and}\qquad H_a:\sigma>2. \end{equation*}
    Equivalently, we are testing \(H_0:\sigma^2=4\) against \(H_a:\sigma^2>4\text{.}\) Because the population is assumed normal, we use the chi-square statistic with \(df=16-1=15\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \chi^2=\frac{(16-1)(2.9)^2}{2^2}=31.5375\approx 31.54. \end{equation*}
    If \(H_0\) is true, then \(W\sim \chi^2[15]\text{.}\) Since this is a right-tailed test, the p-value is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} P(W\ge 31.54)\approx 0.0074. \end{equation*}
    Because \(0.0074<0.05\text{,}\) we reject \(H_0\text{.}\) The sample gives strong evidence that delivery times vary more than the claimed 2 minutes.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 11.8.2. Testing Whether a Machine’s Variation Has Changed.

    Assume bottle fill amounts are normally distributed. A machine is supposed to have population standard deviation 1.2 ounces. A random sample of 12 bottles gives sample standard deviation \(s=1.8\) ounces. Test at the 5% significance level whether the standard deviation differs from 1.2 ounces.
    🔗
    We test
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} H_0:\sigma=1.2 \qquad\text{and}\qquad H_a:\sigma\ne 1.2. \end{equation*}
    Equivalently, \(H_0:\sigma^2=1.44\) and \(H_a:\sigma^2\ne 1.44\text{.}\) The test statistic has \(df=12-1=11\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \chi^2=\frac{(12-1)(1.8)^2}{(1.2)^2}=24.75. \end{equation*}
    For a two-tailed test with \(\alpha=0.05\) and \(df=11\text{,}\) the rejection region is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \chi^2<\chi^2_{0.975}[11] \qquad\text{or}\qquad \chi^2>\chi^2_{0.025}[11]. \end{equation*}
    From Appendix E,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \chi^2_{0.975}[11]\approx 3.816 \qquad\text{and}\qquad \chi^2_{0.025}[11]\approx 21.920. \end{equation*}
    Since \(24.75>21.920\text{,}\) the observed statistic falls in the rejection region, so we reject \(H_0\text{.}\) The data suggest that the machine’s standard deviation differs from 1.2 ounces, and in fact it appears to be larger.
    🔗
    🔗
    The connection with confidence intervals is the same as before. If a claimed value of \(\sigma\) or \(\sigma^2\) falls outside the matching confidence interval from Section 10.4, then that claim is also likely to be rejected by a hypothesis test at the corresponding significance level.
    🔗
