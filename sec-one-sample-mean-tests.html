    Section 11.3 One-Sample Tests for a Mean

    For a one-sample test of a mean, the test statistic depends on the setting:
    🔗
    • Use a z-test when the population standard deviation is known, or when the sample is large enough that using \(s\) in its place is reasonable.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Use a t-test when the sample is small, the population is approximately normal, and the population standard deviation is unknown.
      🔗
      🔗
    To see why the decision rule makes sense, it helps to focus on one concrete case first: a right-tailed z-test. Suppose we want to test
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} H_0:\mu=\mu_0 \qquad\text{versus}\qquad H_a:\mu>\mu_0. \end{equation*}
    Assume the population standard deviation \(\sigma\) is known, so the test statistic is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} Z=\frac{\overline{X}-\mu_0}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}}. \end{equation*}
    If \(H_0\) is true, then the sample mean is centered at \(\mu_0\text{,}\) so
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} Z\sim N(0,1). \end{equation*}
    That tells us what ordinary sampling variation looks like when the null hypothesis is true. Values of \(Z\) near 0 are routine. Moderately positive values can happen by chance. But very large positive values are rare under \(H_0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    This is why a right-tailed test rejects only for large positive statistics. If the alternative says \(\mu>\mu_0\text{,}\) then evidence against \(H_0\) should come from a sample mean that is unusually high, not from one that is low or merely a little above \(\mu_0\text{.}\) The word “large” here means large relative to the null distribution, or in more practical language, many standard errors above \(\mu_0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    At significance level \(\alpha\text{,}\) we agree in advance to reject \(H_0\) only when the observed statistic lands in the most extreme right-tail area \(\alpha\) of the null distribution. That cutoff is the critical value \(z_{\alpha}\text{,}\) defined by
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} P(Z>z_{\alpha})=\alpha. \end{equation*}
    So the rejection rule for a right-tailed z-test is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \text{reject }H_0 \text{ if } z>z_{\alpha}. \end{equation*}
    This rule is sensible for two reasons. First, it rejects only when the sample mean is unusually large under \(H_0\text{.}\) Second, if \(H_0\) really is true, the probability of rejecting is exactly the chosen significance level \(\alpha\text{.}\) In other words, the rule controls the Type I error rate.
    🔗

    Example 11.3.2. Why the Critical-Value and P-Value Rules Agree.

    Suppose a school wants to know whether the mean weekly study time is greater than 40 hours. Assume the population standard deviation is known to be 8 hours, and a random sample of \(n=64\) students gives sample mean \(\overline{x}=42\text{.}\) We test
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} H_0:\mu=40 \qquad\text{versus}\qquad H_a:\mu>40 \end{equation*}
    at significance level \(\alpha=0.05\text{.}\)
    🔗
    If \(H_0\) is true, then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} Z=\frac{\overline{X}-40}{8/\sqrt{64}} =\frac{\overline{X}-40}{1} \sim N(0,1). \end{equation*}
    The observed test statistic is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} z=\frac{42-40}{8/\sqrt{64}}=2. \end{equation*}
    Using the critical-value method, the right-tailed cutoff is \(z_{0.05}=1.645\text{.}\) Since \(2>1.645\text{,}\) the observed statistic lies in the rejection region shown in Figure 11.3.1, so we reject \(H_0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Now look at the same decision through the p-value method. The p-value is the probability, assuming \(H_0\) is true, of getting a test statistic at least as large as the one observed:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \text{p-value}=P(Z\ge 2)\approx 0.0228. \end{equation*}
    This means that if the true mean really were 40, then a sample mean this high or higher would occur only about 2.28% of the time. Since \(0.0228<0.05\text{,}\) the sample is too unusual under \(H_0\text{,}\) so we again reject \(H_0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Figure 11.3.3 explains why the p-value method says the same thing as the critical-value method. In a right-tailed z-test, if the observed statistic is \(z_{\text{obs}}\text{,}\) then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \text{p-value}=P(Z\ge z_{\text{obs}}). \end{equation*}
    The farther to the right \(z_{\text{obs}}\) is, the smaller this tail area becomes. Therefore, for a right-tailed test,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} z_{\text{obs}}>z_{\alpha} \qquad\Longleftrightarrow\qquad P(Z\ge z_{\text{obs}})<P(Z\ge z_{\alpha})=\alpha. \end{equation*}
    So the two rules are really the same comparison written in two different ways:
    🔗
    • The critical-value method compares the observed statistic with a cutoff chosen in advance.
      🔗
      🔗
    • The p-value method compares the observed tail area with the significance level \(\alpha\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    Both methods ask the same question: if \(H_0\) were true, would this sample result be so far out in the right tail that it should count as unusually strong evidence for \(H_a\text{?}\)
    🔗

    Example 11.3.4. A Left-Tailed Test for Battery Life.

    A sample of 50 batteries has mean lifetime \(\overline{x}=21.8\) hours and sample standard deviation \(s=0.9\) hours. Test
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} H_0:\mu=22 \qquad\text{versus}\qquad H_a:\mu<22 \end{equation*}
    at significance level \(\alpha=0.10\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Because the sample is large, we use the z statistic
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} z=\frac{21.8-22}{0.9/\sqrt{50}}\approx -1.57. \end{equation*}
    The p-value is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} P(Z\le -1.57)\approx 0.0582. \end{equation*}
    Since \(0.0582<0.10\text{,}\) we reject \(H_0\text{.}\) At the 10% significance level, the sample gives enough evidence to conclude that the mean battery life is less than 22 hours.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 11.3.5. A Two-Tailed T-Test.

    Ten participants estimate a target distance that is actually 2.5 feet. The sample results have mean \(\overline{x}=2.3\) and sample standard deviation \(s=0.2449\text{.}\) We test
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} H_0:\mu=2.5 \qquad\text{versus}\qquad H_a:\mu\ne 2.5 \end{equation*}
    at the 5% significance level.
    🔗
    Because the sample is small and \(\sigma\) is unknown, we use a t-test with \(df=9\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} t=\frac{2.3-2.5}{0.2449/\sqrt{10}}\approx -2.582. \end{equation*}
    For a two-tailed test with \(\alpha=0.05\) and \(df=9\text{,}\) the critical values are about \(\pm 2.262\text{.}\) Since \(-2.582\) falls beyond the left critical value, we reject \(H_0\text{.}\) The data suggest that the true mean estimate differs from 2.5 feet.
    🔗
    🔗
    Whether we use a critical-value method or a p-value method, the logic is always the same: compare the observed statistic with what the null hypothesis predicts, then decide whether the result is too extreme to be explained by ordinary sampling variation alone.
    🔗
