    Section 9.3 The z-Alpha Notation

    When working with the standard normal distribution, we often use the notation \(z_{\alpha}\text{.}\) This is especially important later when we study confidence intervals and hypothesis tests.
    🔗
    By convention, \(z_{\alpha}\) is the value on the horizontal axis so that the area to the right under the standard normal curve is \(\alpha\text{.}\) In symbols,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} P(Z>z_{\alpha})=\alpha. \end{equation*}
    Since the total area under the curve is 1, the area to the left of \(z_{\alpha}\) is therefore \(1-\alpha\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} P(Z\le z_{\alpha})=1-\alpha. \end{equation*}
    This is the standard convention used in statistics. For example, \(z_{0.025}\approx 1.96\) because the area to the right of 1.96 under the standard normal curve is 0.025.
    🔗
    So if you know the confidence level or the significance level, you can translate that information into a cutoff on the standard normal curve by finding the appropriate \(z_{\alpha}\text{.}\)
    🔗
