    Chapter 8 Binomial Distributions

    The binomial distribution is probably the most common discrete probability distribution in an introductory statistics course. It appears whenever we repeat the same yes-or-no kind of trial and count how many successes occur. Did a shot go in or not? Did a customer buy the product or not? Did a tested item pass inspection or fail?
    🔗
    This chapter explains where the binomial distribution comes from, how to compute its probabilities, how to use its cumulative distribution, how to find its mean and standard deviation, and when a binomial model starts to look like a normal one. Along the way we will keep one main idea in view: a binomial random variable is just a count of successes in repeated independent trials.

    Summary.

    The binomial distribution models a very common kind of random process: repeated independent trials with the same success probability. Because of that, it shows up all over statistics, quality control, polling, biology, and the social sciences. It also gives us a first glimpse of why bell-shaped normal curves will matter in the next chapter.
    🔗
    • A binomial random variable counts the number of successes in \(n\) independent Bernoulli trials.
      🔗
      🔗
    • The binomial setting requires a fixed number of trials, two outcomes per trial, independence, and a constant success probability \(p\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • The probability formula is \(P(X=k)=\binom{n}{k}p^k(1-p)^{n-k}\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • The binomial coefficient \(\binom{n}{k}\) counts the number of ways to place \(k\) successes among \(n\) trials.
      🔗
      🔗
    • The CDF is \(F_X(k)=P(X\le k)\text{,}\) and it helps with “at most,” “at least,” and interval probabilities.
      🔗
      🔗
    • For \(X\sim B(n,p)\text{,}\) the mean is \(np\text{,}\) the variance is \(np(1-p)\text{,}\) and the standard deviation is \(\sqrt{np(1-p)}\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • When \(np\ge 5\) and \(n(1-p)\ge 5\text{,}\) the binomial distribution is often close enough to a normal model with the same mean and standard deviation: \(N(np,\sqrt{np(1-p)})\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    Once you can recognize a binomial situation, most calculations reduce to choosing the right value of \(n\text{,}\) the right value of \(p\text{,}\) and the right way to phrase the event you want. The normal approximation is the bridge from this chapter to the next one, where that bell-shaped model becomes a major tool.
