    Section 8.4 Binomial Approximation of Normal Distribution

    The previous section showed that if \(X\sim B(n,p)\text{,}\) then the mean of \(X\) is \(np\) and the standard deviation is \(\sqrt{np(1-p)}\text{.}\) Those same two numbers will guide us here.
    🔗
    We have not formally studied the normal distribution yet. That happens in Chapter 9. For now, think of a normal distribution as a smooth bell-shaped curve determined by its center and its spread.
    🔗
    When \(n\) is fairly large and \(p\) is not too close to 0 or 1, the graph of a binomial distribution begins to look bell-shaped instead of strongly skewed. A common rule of thumb is that the binomial model \(B(n,p)\) is reasonably approximated by a normal distribution when
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} np\ge 5 \qquad\text{and}\qquad n(1-p)\ge 5. \end{equation*}
    These conditions say that we expect at least about 5 successes and at least about 5 failures. If one side is too small, then the binomial distribution is usually too lopsided for a normal curve to match it well.
    🔗
    When the rule of thumb is satisfied, we match the binomial model with the normal model
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} B(n,p)\approx N(np,\sqrt{np(1-p)}). \end{equation*}
    In other words, we use a normal distribution with the same mean \(np\) and the same standard deviation \(\sqrt{np(1-p)}\) as the binomial distribution. So the formulas from the previous section are exactly what make this approximation possible.
    🔗
    For now, exact binomial probabilities still come from the binomial formula or technology. The point of this section is to see the shape connection. In the next chapter, once area under a normal curve has been introduced, we will be able to use this idea more fully.
    🔗

    Example 8.4.1. A Binomial Model That Looks Nearly Normal.

    Let \(X\sim B(20,0.5)\text{.}\) Then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} np=20(0.5)=10 \qquad\text{and}\qquad \sqrt{np(1-p)}=\sqrt{20(0.5)(0.5)}=\sqrt{5}\approx 2.236. \end{equation*}
    Since \(np=10\) and \(n(1-p)=10\text{,}\) the rule of thumb is satisfied. So \(X\) should be approximated reasonably well by the normal model
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} N(10,\sqrt{5}). \end{equation*}
    Figure 8.4.2 overlays a histogram-style plot of the binomial probabilities with the matching normal curve. The bars rise toward the middle and then fall in a nearly symmetric way, which is exactly the shape we want before using a normal curve as an approximation.
    🔗
    🔗
    In Figure 8.4.2, the binomial bars and the normal curve line up at the same center, and their spreads are very similar. That is exactly what we expect, because both models use mean 10 and standard deviation \(\sqrt{5}\text{.}\)
    🔗
