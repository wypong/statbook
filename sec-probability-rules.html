    Section 6.2 Probability Rules

    A probability law assigns a number \(P(A)\) to each event \(A\text{.}\) The number \(P(A)\) measures the chance that the outcome lies in \(A\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Every probability law satisfies three basic facts.
    🔗
    • \(P(A)\ge 0\) for every event \(A\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • \(P(S)=1\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • If \(A\) and \(B\) are mutually exclusive, then \(P(A\cup B)=P(A)+P(B)\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    From these facts we get several rules that are used all the time:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} 0\le P(A)\le 1, \qquad P(A^c)=1-P(A), \qquad P(A\cup B)=P(A)+P(B)-P(A\cap B). \end{equation*}

    Example 6.2.1. Probability with Equally Likely Outcomes.

    When a fair die is rolled, all six outcomes are equally likely. If \(A=\{2,4,6\}\text{,}\) then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} P(A)=\frac{3}{6}=\frac{1}{2}. \end{equation*}
    If \(B=\{5,6\}\text{,}\) then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} P(B)=\frac{2}{6}=\frac{1}{3}. \end{equation*}
    Since \(A\cap B=\{6\}\text{,}\) we have
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} P(A\cup B)=\frac{3}{6}+\frac{2}{6}-\frac{1}{6}=\frac{4}{6}=\frac{2}{3}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    When the individual outcomes are equally likely, the probability of an event is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} P(A)=\frac{|A|}{|S|}. \end{equation*}
    That shortcut is useful, but it only works in equally likely situations. A loaded die is a good reminder that counting outcomes is not always enough.
    🔗

    Example 6.2.2. A Loaded Die.

    Suppose a loaded die has the probability distribution shown in Table 6.2.3.
    🔗
    🔗
    Let \(A=\{1,3,5\}\) be the event “an odd outcome occurs,” and let \(B=\{3,4,5\}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} P(A)=P(1)+P(3)+P(5)=\frac{2}{5}+\frac{1}{15}+\frac{1}{5}=\frac{2}{3}, \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} P(B)=P(3)+P(4)+P(5)=\frac{1}{15}+\frac{1}{15}+\frac{1}{5}=\frac{1}{3}, \end{equation*}
    and since \(A\cap B=\{3,5\}\text{,}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} P(A\cap B)=\frac{1}{15}+\frac{1}{5}=\frac{4}{15}. \end{equation*}
    Therefore
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} P(A\cup B)=P(A)+P(B)-P(A\cap B)=\frac{2}{3}+\frac{1}{3}-\frac{4}{15}=\frac{11}{15}. \end{equation*}
    Notice what happens here: the event \(\{3,5\}\) contains two outcomes, but its probability is not \(2/6\text{.}\) That is why the formula \(|A|/|S|\) cannot be used unless the outcomes are equally likely.
    🔗
