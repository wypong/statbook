    Section 10.2 Confidence Intervals for the Mean

    A sample mean \(\overline{x}\) is a point estimate of the population mean \(\mu\text{.}\) But a single number does not tell us how much uncertainty comes from sampling. A \(C\) confidence interval adds a margin of error to the point estimate and gives a range of plausible values for \(\mu\text{,}\) where \(0 \lt C \lt 1\) is the confidence level. In informal language, we say we are \(C\) confident that the resulting interval captures the true mean.
    🔗
    To see where the formula comes from, suppose \(X_1,\ldots,X_n\) are iid with mean \(\mu\) and standard deviation \(\sigma\text{,}\) and suppose the standardized sample mean has a standard normal distribution (exactly when the population is normal, or approximately for large samples by the CLT):
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\overline{X}-\mu}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}} \sim N(0,1). \end{equation*}
    Let \(\alpha=1-C\text{.}\) To capture the middle proportion \(C\) of the standard normal curve, we leave total area \(\alpha\) outside and split it evenly between the two tails. That is, each tail has area \(\alpha/2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    So the corresponding probability statement is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} P\left(-z_{\alpha/2} \lt \frac{\overline{X}-\mu}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}} \lt z_{\alpha/2}\right)=C. \end{equation*}
    After we collect the sample and observe \(\overline{x}\text{,}\) we solve that inequality for \(\mu\text{.}\) The result is the z-interval
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \overline{x}\pm z_{\alpha/2}\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}, \end{equation*}
    or, written as an interval,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \left(\overline{x}-z_{\alpha/2}\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}},\; \overline{x}+z_{\alpha/2}\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\right). \end{equation*}
    In practice, \(\sigma\) is often unknown. When the sample is large, we usually replace \(\sigma\) by the sample standard deviation \(s\) and still use the z critical value as an approximation. When the sample is small and the population is normal, we use a z-interval if \(\sigma\) is known, but if \(\sigma\) is unknown and we replace it by \(s\text{,}\) then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\overline{X}-\mu}{s/\sqrt{n}} \sim t[n-1]. \end{equation*}
    In every case, the confidence interval has the form point estimate \(\pm\) margin of error. For the population mean, that means
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} (\overline{x}-\text{margin of error},\overline{x}+\text{margin of error}), \end{equation*}
    So the interval is centered at a sample mean, and its total width is twice the margin of error.
    🔗
    We summarize the confidence-interval settings for a population mean in Table 10.2.2.
    🔗
    The next four examples illustrate these four cases.
    🔗

    Example 10.2.3. Case 1: Large Sample, \(\sigma\) Known.

    Suppose a sample of 36 women has mean height \(\overline{x}=65.1\) inches. Assume the population standard deviation is known to be 2.8 inches. A 95% confidence interval for the mean height is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} 65.1\pm 1.96\frac{2.8}{\sqrt{36}}=65.1\pm 0.9147. \end{equation*}
    So the interval is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} (64.1853,66.0147). \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Example 10.2.4. Case 2: Large Sample, \(\sigma\) Unknown.

    A random sample of 64 delivery times has mean \(\overline{x}=72.4\) minutes and sample standard deviation \(s=12\) minutes. Find a 95% confidence interval for the population mean.
    🔗
    Because the sample is large, we use the z critical value and replace \(\sigma\) by \(s\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \overline{x}\pm z_{0.025}\frac{s}{\sqrt{n}} =72.4\pm 1.96\frac{12}{\sqrt{64}} =72.4\pm 2.94. \end{equation*}
    So the confidence interval is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} (69.46,75.34). \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Example 10.2.5. Case 3: Small Sample, Population Normal, \(\sigma\) Known.

    Assume the population distribution is normal. A sample of size 16 has mean \(\overline{x}=50\text{,}\) and the population standard deviation is known to be \(\sigma=4\text{.}\) Find a 90% confidence interval for the population mean.
    🔗
    Because the population is normal and \(\sigma\) is known, we use a z interval:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \overline{x}\pm z_{0.05}\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} =50\pm 1.645\frac{4}{\sqrt{16}} =50\pm 1.645. \end{equation*}
    So the confidence interval is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} (48.355,51.645). \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Example 10.2.6. Case 4: Small Sample, Population Normal, \(\sigma\) Unknown.

    Assume the population is normal. A sample of 11 observations has sample mean \(\overline{x}=8.8636\) and sample standard deviation \(s=1.3223\text{.}\) For a 95% confidence interval, we use \(df=10\) and \(t_{0.025}[10]=2.228\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The margin of error is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} 2.228\frac{1.3223}{\sqrt{11}}\approx 0.8883. \end{equation*}
    So the confidence interval is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} (8.8636-0.8883,\;8.8636+0.8883)=(7.9753,9.7519). \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Interpreting a confidence interval takes some care. A 95% confidence level does not mean there is a 95% chance that \(\mu\) is in the one interval we already computed. Instead, it means that if we repeated the same sampling process many times and built an interval each time, about 95% of those intervals would capture the true mean. The parameter \(\mu\) is fixed; the interval is what changes from sample to sample.
    🔗
