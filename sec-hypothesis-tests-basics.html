    Section 11.1 Null Hypotheses and P-Values

    A hypothesis test begins with two competing statements.
    🔗
    • The null hypothesis, written \(H_0\text{,}\) is the claim we test against.
      🔗
      🔗
    • The alternative hypothesis, written \(H_a\text{,}\) is what we will support if the data give enough evidence against \(H_0\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    For tests about a population mean, the null hypothesis usually has the form
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} H_0:\mu=\mu_0. \end{equation*}
    The alternative can take one of three common forms:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} H_a:\mu<\mu_0,\qquad H_a:\mu>\mu_0,\qquad H_a:\mu\ne\mu_0. \end{equation*}
    These correspond to a left-tailed test, a right-tailed test, and a two-tailed test.
    🔗
    The next ingredient is the test statistic, a standardized number that measures how far the sample result is from the null value. For one-sample tests of a mean, we use
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} z=\frac{\overline{x}-\mu_0}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}} \qquad\text{or}\qquad t=\frac{\overline{x}-\mu_0}{s/\sqrt{n}}, \end{equation*}
    depending on whether a z-test or t-test is appropriate.
    🔗
    The significance level, written \(\alpha\text{,}\) is the cutoff we use for deciding whether the evidence is strong enough. Common choices are 0.10, 0.05, and 0.01.
    🔗
    One way to make the decision is by using a p-value. The p-value is the probability, assuming \(H_0\) is true, of getting a test statistic at least as extreme as the one observed.
    🔗
    The smaller the p-value, the stronger the evidence against \(H_0\text{.}\) The decision rule is simple:
    🔗
    • If the p-value is less than \(\alpha\text{,}\) reject \(H_0\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • If the p-value is at least \(\alpha\text{,}\) fail to reject \(H_0\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    Notice the wording. We say fail to reject, not “accept \(H_0\text{.}\)” A nonsignificant result does not prove the null hypothesis is true; it only says the sample did not provide enough evidence against it.
    🔗

    Example 11.1.1. Setting Up a Test.

    A manufacturer claims that its batteries last 22 hours on average. A buyer suspects the mean lifetime is actually lower than 22 hours. The hypotheses are
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} H_0:\mu=22 \qquad\text{and}\qquad H_a:\mu<22. \end{equation*}
    Because the alternative says “less than,” this is a left-tailed test.
    🔗
    🔗

    Activity 11.1.1. Hypothesis Claim Match-Up.

    Translate informal claims into null and alternative hypotheses, then identify the tail of the test.
    🔗

    (a)

    A coffee shop claims the average wait time is 5 minutes, but a customer thinks it is longer. Write \(H_0\) and \(H_a\text{,}\) and say whether the test is left-tailed, right-tailed, or two-tailed.
    🔗
    🔗

    (b)

    A school claims the mean math score is 70, and a teacher wants to know whether the mean has changed. Write \(H_0\) and \(H_a\text{,}\) and identify the tail type.
    🔗
    🔗

    (c)

    A snack company claims its bags contain 12 ounces on average, and a buyer suspects the bags are short. Write the hypotheses for that situation.
    🔗
    🔗

    🔗

    At significance level \(\alpha=0.05\text{,}\) decide what to do with p-values 0.12, 0.03, and 0.001.

    For each p-value, explain in one sentence what the decision means in plain language.
