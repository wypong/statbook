    Section 11.5 Paired-Sample Tests for a Mean Difference

    Sometimes two measurements are linked before the data are collected. A person may be measured before and after a treatment, a student may take two versions of a test, or two similar subjects may be matched into a pair. In cases like these, the observations are paired data, and treating the two columns as independent samples would throw away useful information.
    🔗
    The fix is simple: subtract within each pair first. If the paired observations are
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} (x_1,y_1), (x_2,y_2),\ldots,(x_n,y_n), \end{equation*}
    define the individual differences
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} d_i=x_i-y_i. \end{equation*}
    Now the parameter is the population mean difference \(\mu_d\text{.}\) The usual null hypothesis is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} H_0:\mu_d=0, \end{equation*}
    which says there is no average difference between the two paired measurements. More generally, we may test \(H_0:\mu_d=d_0\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Subsection Large Samples: A Z-Test on Differences

    Once we form the differences, this is just a one-sample problem. The sample mean of the differences is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \overline{d}=\frac{d_1+d_2+\cdots+d_n}{n}, \end{equation*}
    and the sample standard deviation of the differences is \(s_d\text{.}\) For large \(n\text{,}\) the CLT says that \(\overline{D}\) is approximately normal with mean \(\mu_d\) and standard deviation \(\sigma_d/\sqrt{n}\text{.}\) Since \(\sigma_d\) is usually unknown, we estimate it with \(s_d\text{.}\)
    🔗
    That gives the large-sample paired z statistic
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} z=\frac{\overline{d}-d_0}{s_d/\sqrt{n}}. \end{equation*}
    For the common null hypothesis \(H_0:\mu_d=0\text{,}\) this becomes
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} z=\frac{\overline{d}}{s_d/\sqrt{n}} =\frac{\overline{d}\sqrt{n}}{s_d}. \end{equation*}
    This formula has the same shape as every other test statistic in this chapter: observed estimate minus null value, divided by its estimated standard error. The only new step is that the observed estimate is the mean of the within-pair differences, not the difference between two independent sample means.
    🔗

    Example 11.5.1. A Large-Sample Paired Test.

    A transit planner records commute times for 40 riders before and after a route change. For each rider, let \(d=\text{before}-\text{after}\text{,}\) so positive differences mean the commute became shorter. The sample has \(\overline{d}=3.2\) minutes and \(s_d=9.0\) minutes. Test whether the route change reduced average commute time:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} H_0:\mu_d=0 \qquad\text{versus}\qquad H_a:\mu_d>0. \end{equation*}
    Because the sample of differences is large, we use
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} z=\frac{3.2-0}{9.0/\sqrt{40}}\approx 2.25. \end{equation*}
    The right-tailed p-value is \(P(Z\ge 2.25)\approx 0.0122\text{.}\) At the 5% significance level, we reject \(H_0\text{.}\) The paired data give evidence that the route change reduced the mean commute time.
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Small Samples: A Paired T-Test

    For small paired samples, we still subtract within each pair and test the mean of the differences. The statistic is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} t=\frac{\overline{d}-d_0}{s_d/\sqrt{n}}. \end{equation*}
    For \(H_0:\mu_d=0\text{,}\) this is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} t=\frac{\overline{d}\sqrt{n}}{s_d}. \end{equation*}
    The reason for the t distribution is the same as in a one-sample t-test. With a small sample, \(s_d\) is a noisy estimate of the true standard deviation of the differences, so the standardized statistic has heavier tails than the standard normal curve. Since there are \(n\) differences, the degrees of freedom are
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} df=n-1. \end{equation*}
    This is called a paired-sample t-test. It is not a two-sample t-test. After forming the differences, we have one sample: \(d_1,\ldots,d_n\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The conditions are that the pairs are randomly selected or otherwise representative, the pairs are independent of each other, and the distribution of differences is approximately normal. For larger samples, the CLT makes the normality condition less strict. For small samples, strong skewness or serious outliers in the differences can make the paired t-test unreliable.
    🔗

    Example 11.5.3. A Small-Sample Paired T-Test.

    A class records systolic blood pressure for 10 volunteers before and after a relaxation exercise. Let \(d=\text{before}-\text{after}\text{,}\) so positive differences mean blood pressure decreased. The differences have \(\overline{d}=4.6\) and \(s_d=5.8\text{.}\) Test whether the exercise lowers the mean blood pressure:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} H_0:\mu_d=0 \qquad\text{versus}\qquad H_a:\mu_d>0. \end{equation*}
    The test statistic is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} t=\frac{4.6-0}{5.8/\sqrt{10}}\approx 2.51. \end{equation*}
    There are \(df=10-1=9\) degrees of freedom. For a right-tailed test, technology gives a p-value of about \(0.017\text{.}\) At the 5% significance level, we reject \(H_0\text{.}\) The sample gives evidence that the exercise lowers mean systolic blood pressure, assuming the differences are reasonably close to normal.
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Paired or Independent?

    The choice between a paired test and an independent two-sample test is not about sample size. It is about how the data were collected. If each observation in one group is naturally linked to one observation in the other group, use the paired differences. If the two groups are separate and unrelated, use an independent two-sample test.
    🔗
