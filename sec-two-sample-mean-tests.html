    Section 11.4 Two-Sample Mean Tests

    Many questions compare two population means rather than one. For example, we might compare the mean exam score for two teaching methods, the mean wait time at two clinics, or the mean amount spent by two groups of customers.
    🔗
    Let \(\mu_1\) and \(\mu_2\) be the two population means. The parameter we care about is the difference of means
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \mu_1-\mu_2. \end{equation*}
    The natural estimate of this difference is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \overline{x}_1-\overline{x}_2. \end{equation*}
    So a typical null hypothesis is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} H_0:\mu_1-\mu_2=0, \end{equation*}
    which says the two population means are equal. The alternative can be left-tailed, right-tailed, or two-tailed:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} H_a:\mu_1-\mu_2<0,\qquad H_a:\mu_1-\mu_2>0,\qquad H_a:\mu_1-\mu_2\ne 0. \end{equation*}

    Subsection Large Samples: A Two-Sample Z-Test

    To see where the test statistic comes from, start with the sampling distributions of the two sample means. For large samples, the CLT gives
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \overline{X}_1\sim N\left(\mu_1,\frac{\sigma_1}{\sqrt{n_1}}\right) \qquad\text{and}\qquad \overline{X}_2\sim N\left(\mu_2,\frac{\sigma_2}{\sqrt{n_2}}\right) \end{equation*}
    approximately. Here the second input in \(N(\mu,\sigma)\) is the standard deviation, so the variances are \(\sigma_1^2/n_1\) and \(\sigma_2^2/n_2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Now use two facts from earlier chapters. First, sums and differences of independent normal random variables are normal. Second, from Section 7.2, expectation is linear and variances add for independent random variables. Therefore the difference
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \overline{X}_1-\overline{X}_2 \end{equation*}
    is approximately normal. Its mean is
    🔗
    \begin{align*} E(\overline{X}_1-\overline{X}_2)&=E(\overline{X}_1)+E(-\overline{X}_2)\\ &=E(\overline{X}_1)-E(\overline{X}_2)\\ &=\mu_1-\mu_2. \end{align*}
    Because the two samples are independent, the variance is
    🔗
    \begin{align*} \operatorname{Var}(\overline{X}_1-\overline{X}_2)&=\operatorname{Var}(\overline{X}_1)+\operatorname{Var}(-\overline{X}_2)\\ &=\operatorname{Var}(\overline{X}_1)+\operatorname{Var}(\overline{X}_2)\\ &=\frac{\sigma_1^2}{n_1}+\frac{\sigma_2^2}{n_2}. \end{align*}
    So, for large independent samples, the difference is approximately
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \overline{X}_1-\overline{X}_2 \sim N\left(\mu_1-\mu_2,\sqrt{\frac{\sigma_1^2}{n_1}+\frac{\sigma_2^2}{n_2}}\right) \end{equation*}
    approximately. This is the key reason the standard error for a difference of sample means is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \sqrt{\frac{\sigma_1^2}{n_1}+\frac{\sigma_2^2}{n_2}}. \end{equation*}
    In practice, the population standard deviations \(\sigma_1\) and \(\sigma_2\) are usually unknown. For large samples, we estimate them with \(s_1\) and \(s_2\text{.}\) That gives the large-sample two-sample z-test statistic
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} z=\frac{(\overline{x}_1-\overline{x}_2)-D_0} {\sqrt{\frac{s_1^2}{n_1}+\frac{s_2^2}{n_2}}}, \end{equation*}
    where \(D_0\) is the difference claimed by the null hypothesis. For the common null hypothesis \(H_0:\mu_1-\mu_2=0\text{,}\) this becomes
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} z=\frac{\overline{x}_1-\overline{x}_2} {\sqrt{\frac{s_1^2}{n_1}+\frac{s_2^2}{n_2}}}. \end{equation*}
    As usual, the z statistic tells us how far the observed result is from the null value, measured in standard errors. A large positive z-value supports an alternative such as \(\mu_1-\mu_2>0\text{.}\) A large negative z-value supports an alternative such as \(\mu_1-\mu_2<0\text{.}\) Values far from 0 in either direction support a two-tailed alternative.
    🔗
    The main conditions are that the two samples are random or otherwise representative, the two samples are independent of each other, and both sample sizes are large enough for the CLT to make the sample means approximately normal. A common rule of thumb is that both \(n_1\) and \(n_2\) should be at least 30, though strongly skewed populations may require larger samples.
    🔗

    Example 11.4.2. Comparing Two Teaching Methods.

    A department compares exam scores from two large independent classes. Method 1 has \(n_1=64\text{,}\) \(\overline{x}_1=78\text{,}\) and \(s_1=10\text{.}\) Method 2 has \(n_2=49\text{,}\) \(\overline{x}_2=74\text{,}\) and \(s_2=9\text{.}\) Test whether Method 1 has the higher mean score:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} H_0:\mu_1-\mu_2=0 \qquad\text{versus}\qquad H_a:\mu_1-\mu_2>0. \end{equation*}
    The estimated standard error is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \sqrt{\frac{10^2}{64}+\frac{9^2}{49}} \approx 1.79. \end{equation*}
    The observed difference is \(78-74=4\text{,}\) so
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} z=\frac{4-0}{1.79}\approx 2.23. \end{equation*}
    For a right-tailed test, the p-value is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} P(Z\ge 2.23)\approx 0.0129. \end{equation*}
    At the 5% significance level, \(0.0129<0.05\text{,}\) so we reject \(H_0\text{.}\) The sample gives evidence that Method 1 has the higher population mean exam score.
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Small Samples: A Two-Sample T-Test

    When one or both samples are small, the same basic comparison is still natural:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \text{observed difference} - \text{null difference}. \end{equation*}
    So the numerator stays
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} (\overline{x}_1-\overline{x}_2)-D_0. \end{equation*}
    The standard error idea also stays the same. The spread of \(\overline{X}_1-\overline{X}_2\) comes from the spread of both sample means, so we still use
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \sqrt{\frac{s_1^2}{n_1}+\frac{s_2^2}{n_2}}. \end{equation*}
    The difference is the reference distribution. With large samples, replacing \(\sigma_1\) and \(\sigma_2\) by \(s_1\) and \(s_2\) barely changes the shape, so a standard normal model works well. With small samples, that extra uncertainty matters more. The test statistic
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} t=\frac{(\overline{x}_1-\overline{x}_2)-D_0} {\sqrt{\frac{s_1^2}{n_1}+\frac{s_2^2}{n_2}}} \end{equation*}
    is compared with a t distribution instead of a standard normal distribution.
    🔗
    There is one complication: this statistic does not usually have a t distribution with a simple degree of freedom like \(n_1+n_2-2\text{.}\) Because the denominator uses two separate sample variances, the degrees of freedom are estimated. A common choice is the Welch approximation:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} df\approx \frac{\left(\frac{s_1^2}{n_1}+\frac{s_2^2}{n_2}\right)^2} {\frac{\left(s_1^2/n_1\right)^2}{n_1-1} +\frac{\left(s_2^2/n_2\right)^2}{n_2-1}}. \end{equation*}
    This formula looks unpleasant, but its purpose is reasonable: it adjusts the t curve for the fact that two sample standard deviations are being estimated, possibly from samples of different sizes and different spreads. In practice, technology usually computes the degrees of freedom. The important idea is that small samples need a t reference curve because the estimated standard error is less stable.
    🔗
    This method is often called Welch’s two-sample t-test. It is a good default because it does not require us to assume the two population variances are equal.
    🔗
    The conditions are stricter than in the large-sample case. The samples should be random or otherwise representative, the two samples should be independent of each other, and each population distribution should be approximately normal or at least not strongly skewed with serious outliers. For very small samples, the normality condition matters a lot.
    🔗

    Example 11.4.4. Comparing Two Small Samples.

    A tutor compares quiz scores from two small independent groups. Group 1 has \(n_1=12\text{,}\) \(\overline{x}_1=84\text{,}\) and \(s_1=6\text{.}\) Group 2 has \(n_2=10\text{,}\) \(\overline{x}_2=79\text{,}\) and \(s_2=7\text{.}\) Test whether the first group has the higher population mean score:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} H_0:\mu_1-\mu_2=0 \qquad\text{versus}\qquad H_a:\mu_1-\mu_2>0. \end{equation*}
    The estimated standard error is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \sqrt{\frac{6^2}{12}+\frac{7^2}{10}} =\sqrt{7.9}\approx 2.81. \end{equation*}
    The test statistic is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} t=\frac{(84-79)-0}{2.81}\approx 1.78. \end{equation*}
    Using the Welch formula gives about \(df=18\text{.}\) For a right-tailed test, technology gives
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \text{p-value}\approx P(T\ge 1.78)\approx 0.046. \end{equation*}
    At the 5% significance level, this is just small enough to reject \(H_0\text{.}\) The sample gives some evidence that the first group has the higher population mean score, assuming the small-sample conditions are reasonable.
    🔗
    🔗
