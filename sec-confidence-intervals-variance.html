    Section 10.4 Confidence Intervals for a Population Variance and Standard Deviation

    Sometimes the main question is about spread rather than center. A manufacturer may want to estimate how much bottle fills vary. A coach may care about the variability of race times, not just the average time. In problems like these, the parameter of interest is the population variance \(\sigma^2\text{,}\) or equivalently the population standard deviation \(\sigma\text{.}\)
    🔗
    From Section 4.3, the sample variance is based on squared deviations from the sample mean. Before the data are collected, the random sample variance is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} S^2=\frac{1}{n-1}\sum_{i=1}^n (X_i-\overline{X})^2. \end{equation*}
    After the sample is observed, its value is written \(s^2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The reason the chi-square distribution appears is that this formula is built from squared normal quantities. Suppose \(X_1,\ldots,X_n\) is a random sample from a normal population \(N(\mu,\sigma)\text{.}\) If we standardize each observation,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} Z_i=\frac{X_i-\mu}{\sigma}, \end{equation*}
    then each \(Z_i\) is standard normal. A sum of squares of independent standard normal random variables has a chi-square distribution.
    🔗
    If the population mean \(\mu\) were known, then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{i=1}^n\left(\frac{X_i-\mu}{\sigma}\right)^2\sim \chi^2_n. \end{equation*}
    But in practice \(\mu\) is usually unknown, so we replace it by the sample mean \(\overline{X}\text{.}\) That uses up one degree of freedom, because once \(n-1\) of the deviations \(X_i-\overline{X}\) are known, the last one is forced by the fact that they add up to 0. So
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{i=1}^n\left(\frac{X_i-\overline{X}}{\sigma}\right)^2\sim \chi^2_{n-1}. \end{equation*}
    Since
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{i=1}^n (X_i-\overline{X})^2=(n-1)S^2, \end{equation*}
    the key result becomes
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \frac{(n-1)S^2}{\sigma^2}\sim \chi^2_{n-1}. \end{equation*}
    That one fact is what makes a confidence interval for \(\sigma^2\) possible.
    🔗
    The assumptions need to be stated carefully.
    🔗
    • The data should come from a random sample or a similarly independent sampling process.
      🔗
      🔗
    • The observations should be independent. If sampling is done without replacement from a finite population, a common rule of thumb is that the population should be at least 10 times as large as the sample.
      🔗
      🔗
    • The population itself should be normal. This is the crucial assumption.
      🔗
      🔗
    That last point matters more here than it did for intervals about a mean. The CLT helps sample means become approximately normal for large samples, but it does not make the chi-square interval for variance exact. For variance and standard deviation, this method is justified only when the population distribution is normal or very close to normal.
    🔗
    Let \(C=1-\alpha\text{.}\) Using the critical-value notation from Appendix E, where \(\chi^2_{\alpha}[n-1]\) leaves right-tail area \(\alpha\) for a chi-square distribution with \(n-1\) degrees of freedom, we have
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} P\left( \chi^2_{1-\alpha/2}[n-1] \le \frac{(n-1)S^2}{\sigma^2} \le \chi^2_{\alpha/2}[n-1] \right)=C. \end{equation*}
    Solving that inequality for \(\sigma^2\) gives the confidence interval for the population variance:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \left( \frac{(n-1)s^2}{\chi^2_{\alpha/2}[n-1]}, \frac{(n-1)s^2}{\chi^2_{1-\alpha/2}[n-1]} \right). \end{equation*}
    If we want an interval for the population standard deviation instead, we simply take square roots of both endpoints:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \left( \sqrt{\frac{(n-1)s^2}{\chi^2_{\alpha/2}[n-1]}}, \sqrt{\frac{(n-1)s^2}{\chi^2_{1-\alpha/2}[n-1]}} \right). \end{equation*}

    Example 10.4.1. An Interval for Variation in Delivery Times.

    Suppose delivery times on a short route are well modeled by a normal distribution. A random sample of 12 deliveries has sample standard deviation \(s=4\) minutes. Find a 95% confidence interval for the population variance and the population standard deviation.
    🔗
    Here \(n=12\text{,}\) so the degrees of freedom are \(n-1=11\text{.}\) For 95% confidence, \(\alpha=0.05\text{.}\) From Appendix E,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \chi^2_{0.025}[11]\approx 21.920 \qquad\text{and}\qquad \chi^2_{0.975}[11]\approx 3.816. \end{equation*}
    Since \(s^2=16\text{,}\) the variance interval is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \left( \frac{11(16)}{21.920}, \frac{11(16)}{3.816} \right) \approx (8.03,46.12). \end{equation*}
    Now take square roots to get an interval for the population standard deviation:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} (\sqrt{8.03},\sqrt{46.12})\approx (2.83,6.79). \end{equation*}
    So a reasonable estimate for the route-to-route spread is that the population standard deviation is between about 2.83 and 6.79 minutes.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 10.4.2. Why Large Sample Size Is Not the Main Issue.

    A researcher takes a random sample of 50 household incomes and wants a confidence interval for the population variance. Even though \(n=50\) is not small, household incomes are usually strongly right-skewed.
    🔗
    So a chi-square interval for \(\sigma^2\) is not automatically justified. The key result \((n-1)S^2/\sigma^2\sim\chi^2_{n-1}\) depends on a normal population, not just on a large sample. For variance intervals, the shape of the population matters a lot.
    🔗
