    Section 11.4 Tests for a Population Proportion

    This section is the testing counterpart to Section 10.3. The setup is the same: each observation is a success or failure, the population proportion of success is \(p\text{,}\) and the sample proportion is \(\widehat{p}=X/n\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The normal model behind proportion tests comes from Section 8.4. If \(X\sim B(n,p)\text{,}\) then for large enough \(n\text{,}\) the count \(X\) is approximately normal. Dividing by \(n\) gives an approximate normal model for \(\widehat{p}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    To test a claim about a population proportion, we begin with hypotheses such as
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} H_0:p=p_0 \qquad\text{and}\qquad H_a:p<p_0,\quad p>p_0,\quad\text{or}\quad p\ne p_0. \end{equation*}
    Under the null hypothesis, the sample proportion is approximately normal with mean \(p_0\) and standard deviation
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \sqrt{\frac{p_0(1-p_0)}{n}}, \end{equation*}
    provided the expected counts under \(H_0\) are both at least about 5:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} np_0\ge 5 \qquad\text{and}\qquad n(1-p_0)\ge 5. \end{equation*}
    That leads to the one-proportion z-test statistic
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} z=\frac{\widehat{p}-p_0}{\sqrt{p_0(1-p_0)/n}}. \end{equation*}
    This looks a lot like the z-interval from Section 10.3, but there is an important difference. In a confidence interval, the unknown proportion \(p\) is replaced by \(\widehat{p}\) in the standard error. In a hypothesis test, the null hypothesis tells us exactly which value to use, so the standard error is built from \(p_0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Once the z statistic is computed, the rest is familiar:
    🔗
    • For \(H_a:p<p_0\text{,}\) use the left-tail p-value \(P(Z\le z)\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • For \(H_a:p>p_0\text{,}\) use the right-tail p-value \(P(Z\ge z)\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • For \(H_a:p\ne p_0\text{,}\) use the two-tail p-value \(2P(Z\ge |z|)\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    As before, reject \(H_0\) when the p-value is less than the chosen significance level \(\alpha\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Example 11.4.1. Testing a Claimed Defect Rate.

    A manufacturer claims that at most 4% of its light bulbs are defective. A quality-control inspector checks 250 bulbs and finds 18 defectives. Is there evidence at the 5% significance level that the true defect rate is higher than 4%?
    🔗
    Let \(p\) be the population proportion of defective bulbs. Then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} H_0:p=0.04 \qquad\text{and}\qquad H_a:p>0.04. \end{equation*}
    The sample proportion is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \widehat{p}=\frac{18}{250}=0.072. \end{equation*}
    The large-sample conditions under the null are satisfied because
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} np_0=250(0.04)=10 \qquad\text{and}\qquad n(1-p_0)=250(0.96)=240. \end{equation*}
    So the test statistic is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} z=\frac{0.072-0.04}{\sqrt{0.04(0.96)/250}}\approx 2.58. \end{equation*}
    Because this is a right-tailed test, the p-value is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} P(Z\ge 2.58)\approx 0.0049. \end{equation*}
    Since \(0.0049<0.05\text{,}\) we reject \(H_0\text{.}\) The sample gives strong evidence that the defect rate is greater than 4%.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 11.4.2. Testing a Claimed Support Level.

    A college newspaper reports that 60% of students support a new parking plan. A random sample of 200 students finds that 108 support the plan. Test the newspaper’s claim at the 5% significance level.
    🔗
    Let \(p\) be the true proportion of all students who support the plan. Since we want to know whether the reported value is off in either direction, we test
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} H_0:p=0.60 \qquad\text{and}\qquad H_a:p\ne 0.60. \end{equation*}
    The sample proportion is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \widehat{p}=\frac{108}{200}=0.54. \end{equation*}
    The null-model counts are
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} np_0=200(0.60)=120 \qquad\text{and}\qquad n(1-p_0)=200(0.40)=80, \end{equation*}
    so the normal approximation is reasonable. The test statistic is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} z=\frac{0.54-0.60}{\sqrt{0.60(0.40)/200}}\approx -1.73. \end{equation*}
    For a two-tailed test, the p-value is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} 2P(Z\ge 1.73)\approx 0.0836. \end{equation*}
    Since \(0.0836>0.05\text{,}\) we fail to reject \(H_0\text{.}\) At the 5% level, the sample does not give enough evidence to conclude that the true support level differs from 60%.
    🔗
    🔗
    The connection with confidence intervals is worth noticing. If a hypothesized value \(p_0\) sits far outside a confidence interval for \(p\text{,}\) then it is also likely to be rejected by a hypothesis test at a matching significance level. So intervals and tests are two ways of looking at the same sample evidence.
    🔗
